Working through core computer science courses since I feel like my knowledge is very spread out in chunks and doesn't really seem to fit together. 

I'm in my final semester of [Systems Design Engineering](https://uwaterloo.ca/systems-design-engineering/) at the UWaterloo. A lot of people unfamiliar with the course offerings in the program tend to think that Systems Design teaches us how to "design software systems" (the typical sys design questions you get asked in interviews). This cannot be further from the truth. In my undergrad degree, I took 2 software related courses that were mandatory - introduction to programming and DSA. The only one I really count is DSA. 

As part of my electives, I took ML, Databases, Mobile Robotics, and Programming for Performance. This was all in my 3B term or beyond. Most of these were taken on exchange as well, so it was difficult optimizing for learning while also focusing on new school, traveling, making memorable experiences. One might argue that DSA is all you need to "get a job" and then you can learn stuff on the job. I do think that there is truth to this statement. I landed 6 internships (2 at Apple), purely based on my DSA knowledge. 

However, there is a fundamental shift happening in the way we learn and the way we do our jobs as swe's. AI can get us 90% of the way there in whatever task we are doing (most of the time all the way), and somewhere between this we have forgotten the real reason we study computer science. It isn't about the actual material, it's about how to learn and how to think. And myself, along with many of my peers feel the same way in that we are unable to think as critically about problems. 

I personally feel like I am not caught up to many CS grads who have taken software engineering course in an academic setting. I'm not sure to what degree that is true, but I personally need to know the fundamentals of whatever I am working on. And the fundamentals for me aren't really that strong. Hence I have taken up some side learning to teach myself CS and really understand core concepts that will strengthen my understanding, regardless of how applicable they are on the job. Knowing how operating systems work, how code is compiled, how it actually runs on a piece of hardware without it feeling like magic, how programming languages actually work internally, why networking is such a big deal, how data is transffered through light?? and much more.

All of these topics feel so out of reach right now and even in an infrastructure role I am starting full-time in July, I need to know how servers work, how VMs work, how code is deployed and what is actually happening on these servers, what do makefiles, dockerfiles work, what does kubernetes actually do, etc etc.. Knowing these fundamental things will help me craft systems carefully, securely, and make me feel satisfied in the work I am doing. I am definitely familiar with a lot of systems and how they interact, however I either have a knowledge gap in a certain area or my knowledge isn't as deep as I want it to be. Regardless of your thoughts or opinions or what is worth spending time on or not, I think this will serve me well in the long run.

I found this online and it seems to cover everything I want to learn: https://teachyourselfcs.com

The section outline is very strong. I don't quite enjoy the particular videos/books that are linked as learning content because either they are very old, or not engaging/intuitive enough for me to understand. I'm going to use the outline to find my own resources and learn each topic in depth.
