title: Teaching Myself Computer Science

I am working through core computer science courses because I feel like my knowledge is spread out in chunks and doesn't quite fit together. 

I'm in my final semester of [Systems Design Engineering](https://uwaterloo.ca/systems-design-engineering/) at UWaterloo. A lot of people unfamiliar with the program assume that Systems Design teaches us how to "design software systems"—the typical systems design questions you get asked in interviews. This couldn't be further from the truth. In my entire undergrad degree, I took exactly two mandatory software-related courses: Introduction to Programming and Data Structures & Algorithms (DSA). Honestly, the only one I really count is DSA. 

As part of my electives, I took Machine Learning, Databases, Mobile Robotics, and Programming for Performance. But this was all in my 3B term or beyond. Most of these were taken on exchange as well, making it difficult to optimize for deep learning while also balancing a new school, traveling, and making memorable experiences. 

One might argue that DSA is all you need to "get a job," and then you can learn the rest on the job. I think there is truth to this statement—I landed six internships (two at Apple) purely based on my DSA knowledge. 

However, there is a fundamental shift happening in the way we learn and the way we do our jobs as software engineers. AI can get us 90% of the way there in almost any task, and somewhere along the line, we have forgotten the real reason we study computer science. It isn't about the actual material; it's about how to learn and how to think. I, along with many of my peers, feel that we are increasingly unable to think critically about deeply technical problems. 

I personally feel like I am not caught up to many CS grads who have taken software engineering courses in a rigorous academic setting. I'm not sure to what degree that is true, but I personally need to know the fundamentals of whatever I am working on. And right now, my fundamentals aren't as strong as I want them to be. 

Hence, I have taken up side-learning to teach myself CS and truly understand the core concepts. Knowing how operating systems work, how code is compiled, how it actually runs on a piece of hardware without feeling like magic, how programming languages work internally, why networking is such a big deal, how data is transferred through light—all of this matters to me, regardless of how immediately applicable it is on the job.

All of these topics feel somewhat out of reach right now. Even for the infrastructure role I am starting full-time in July, I need to know how servers and VMs work, how code is deployed, what is actually happening under the hood, what Makefiles and Dockerfiles do, and what Kubernetes actually is. Knowing these fundamental things will help me craft systems carefully, securely, and make me feel deeply satisfied in the work I am doing. I am definitely familiar with a lot of systems and how they interact, but I either have a knowledge gap in a certain area or my knowledge isn't as deep as I want it to be. 

Regardless of what others think is worth spending time on, I know this will serve me well in the long run.

I found a great curriculum online that seems to cover everything I want to learn: [teachyourselfcs.com](https://teachyourselfcs.com).

The section outline is very strong. I don't quite enjoy the particular videos or books linked there because they are often older or not engaging enough for my learning style. Instead, I'm going to use the outline as a roadmap to find my own resources and learn each topic in depth.

### The Core Curriculum

1. Computer Architecture
2. Algorithms and Data Structures *(going beyond what I already know)*
3. Math for CS *(mostly covered through university)*
4. Operating Systems
5. Computer Networking
6. Databases
7. Languages and Compilers
8. Distributed Systems

### Beyond the Core

1. Machine Learning
2. Robotics
3. Compute Hardware
4. Real-time OS
