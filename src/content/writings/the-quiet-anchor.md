---
title: "Notes on Confidence"
date: 07/2026
---

I often find myself standing squarely between two opposing forces: the quiet, persistent voice of self-doubt on the inside, and the loud, unpredictable noise of criticism on the outside. 

This piece is an attempt to build a relationship with myself where I am not so violently pulled by either.

Recently, I’ve been reading the Bhagavad Gita, and it struck me how Krishna frames the idea of self-belief. He explains that true confidence does not inflate with praise, nor does it collapse under the weight of criticism. Instead, it comes from a much quieter place: knowing your duty and acting on it steadily, without being disturbed by the opinions of others.

That made me realize how often I confuse confidence with ego. 

Ego says, *“I am better than others.”*  
Confidence says, *“I am capable of doing what needs to be done.”*

Ego depends entirely on comparison. Confidence depends on clarity. If I am being entirely honest, a lot of what I have previously called "confidence" was just my ego speaking. And the easiest way to test this is to see what happens when it is challenged. If your confidence disappears the moment you are criticized, it wasn't confidence at all, it was just an ego propped up by external approval.

To start shifting from ego to actual confidence, I've been trying to force three practical habits:

1. **Detach from the immediate reaction.** When criticized, just pause. Don't defend, don't attack.
2. **Check the underlying intention.** Was I acting from a place of insecurity, or from clarity?
3. **Take small, bold actions daily.** Confidence isn't built through looking in the mirror and saying affirmations; it’s built through stacking undeniable evidence that you can do hard things.

But what happens when you try to do hard things and fail? 

I’ve been falling short of my own goals for a while now, and my default emotional response has been a sinking feeling of being inherently incapable. 

Krishna addresses this directly: You have control over the effort, never the outcome.

> *“You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits.”* (2.47)

Failure hurts so much because we unconsciously tie our results directly to our identity. When things go wrong, the mind immediately jumps to *"I failed,"* rather than the much more accurate and useful, *"the method failed."* 

Instead of spiraling, the questions I need to ask myself are clinical: Did I actually give consistent effort? Was the strategy correct? Did I simply underestimate the difficulty of the task? Did I allow fear or distraction to weaken my effort?

The realization here is that my primary task is not to feel "motivated." Motivation is cheap, it can come from a few youtube videos. My task is to build steadiness. 

But *how*? That’s the part I always get stuck on. I don’t put in enough effort, I lose focus easily, and I am deeply attached to my comfort. How do you build steadiness with a mind that constantly rebels against it?

> *“The mind is restless, turbulent, strong and obstinate.”* (6.34)

It helps to realize that a loss of focus is not a character flaw. It is just the natural, default tendency of an untrained mind. The real problem isn't that I lack confidence; the problem is that I am waiting to *feel* confident before taking action. 

The antidote is removing the emotional negotiation. I have to stop asking myself, *"Do I feel like doing this?"* Most times I dont even ask it, my body just defaults to it.

On the other side, I can't just keep mindlessly "doing." I struggle to act when I don't feel a strong emotional connection to the goal I have in mind. 

> *“Even a little progress on this path protects one from great fear.”* (2.40)

I asked myself a difficult question recently: *If nobody judged me and nobody praised me, would I still want the things I am working toward?*

Honestly, I couldn't think of a single goal that survived that filter. Does that mean no audience equals no drive?

It means I haven't built an internal anchor yet. Motivation only becomes stable when it aligns with your fundamental nature (the concept of *svadharma*). 

But purpose is not found by sitting in a room and thinking very hard about it. It is found by engaging with the world. Krishna didn't tell Arjun to sit and discover his ultimate calling; he told him to act according to his nature and observe. 

I’ve spent too much time thinking: *I must know before I act.*  
The shift I need to make is: *I will act to discover.*

If I can just try to become a consistent person, the confidence will follow. The new identity I am trying to chase isn't tied to winning. It's simply: *“I am someone who attempts things even if the outcome is uncertain.”*

The only metric for success that actually matters is: *Did I try seriously?*  
Not: *Did I win?*

The irony of writing all this down is that it creates a sudden surge of motivation, and I easily fall into the trap of thinking my mindset has magically shifted. The reality is that it probably hasn't. Unlearning takes time. Growing up as the "gifted" kid, constantly comparing myself within the intense Waterloo bubble, and subconsciously believing that having to try hard is a signal of weakness. These are deep, stubborn patterns. 

The biggest disservice I can do to myself is letting the ego convince me that I am too smart to need to try. Because the cycle is brutal: you think you're too good to take action, you fail because you didn't act, your ego takes a hit, and you retreat further. The cycle repeats until you are left with neither confidence nor ego. 

Still, putting this on paper helps offload the mental fatigue I’ve been carrying. The thoughts are out of my head, creating a little more space and clarity. And when I inevitably find myself feeling insecure, or unnecessarily arrogant, I have something to come back and read.

To distill everything down to its absolute core, I want to end with a simple chain of questions:

*How do I make sure I actually take action?*  
By developing steadiness and consistency.

*How do I develop consistency when the mind naturally wants to be distracted?*  
By building an internal anchor.

*What makes a good internal anchor?*  
Pursuing goals that exist beyond myself and can never truly be "finished" - like becoming a better thinker, helping others, or mastering a craft, rather than just hitting a temporary metric.

*How do I discover goals that are larger than myself?*  
By lowering the stakes and simply engaging with the world. You cannot think your way into a purpose; you have to stumble into it through action.

*How do I start engaging when I feel paralyzed by the outcome?*  
By accepting that right now, my only job is to try. Not to win, not to be impressive, and not to be perfect. Just to try, and to let that be enough.
